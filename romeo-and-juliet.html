üìñ What Kind of Story Is This?

Big idea: This is not just a love story that goes wrong. It is a tragedy where love tries to exist inside a violent world.
Tragedy

A type of story where the ending is meant to be painful, not accidental. Characters face powerful forces they cannot completely control.

  • Strong emotions collide with strict rules
  • Choices have permanent consequences
  • We are meant to ask: How did this happen?
What You Should Watch For
  • How quickly people make decisions
  • How adults handle conflict
  • How public violence affects private love

üåç The World of the Play

Setting: Verona, Italy, a city obsessed with honor, reputation, and public image.

Social Rules You Need to Know

Public Life
  • Insults often lead to street fights.
  • Honor and reputation matter more than happiness.
  • People pay attention to how you act in public.
Private Life
  • Family loyalty is expected and enforced.
  • Parents control most major decisions, including marriage.
  • Teenagers have strong feelings but little real power.

Before any love story begins, the city is already broken by a long-running feud between two powerful families:

  • Montagues
  • Capulets

Key Facts About the Feud

  • It is an ancient grudge ‚Äì no one even remembers exactly how it started.
  • Children and servants are born into the hatred, even if they did nothing to cause it.
  • Regular citizens (the ‚Äúcivil‚Äù people of Verona) are dragged into the violence.
Essential question: What happens when you inherit a conflict you never chose?

üó£Ô∏è How Shakespeare‚Äôs Language Works

Important truth: You do not need to understand every word to understand the scene.

What to Focus On Instead

  • Who is angry? Who is calm?
  • Which words repeat? (light, dark, stars, fate)
  • When is the language poetic vs. blunt or insulting?

Quick Tools

Iambic Pentameter

Most important lines follow a ‚Äúheartbeat‚Äù rhythm: da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM.

This makes speeches sound formal and musical.

Prose vs. Verse
  • Verse: structured lines, rhythm, poetry; used for serious and emotional moments.
  • Prose: regular speech; used for jokes, servants, and casual talk.

üìú The Prologue (Spoken by the Chorus)

The Prologue is like a movie trailer that tells you the ending first. It is written as a 14-line sonnet.

Original Text ‚Äì Read it out loud slowly

Two households, both alike in dignity,

In fair Verona, where we lay our scene,

From ancient grudge break to new mutiny,

Where civil blood makes civil hands unclean.

From forth the fatal loins of these two foes

A pair of star-cross'd lovers take their life;

Whose misadventured piteous overthrows

Do with their death bury their parents' strife.

The fearful passage of their death-mark'd love,

And the continuance of their parents' rage,

Which, but their children's end, nought could remove,

Is now the two hours' traffic of our stage;

The which if you with patient ears attend,

What here shall miss, our toil shall strive to mend.
